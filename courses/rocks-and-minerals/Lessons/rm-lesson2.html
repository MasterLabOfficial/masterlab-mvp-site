Lesson 2 — Mineral Identification: Physical Properties

MasterLab Rocks & Minerals (Division C)

1. Introduction

Now that you know what a mineral is, the next step is learning how to identify them. Science Olympiad relies heavily on physical properties — measurable, observable traits that help distinguish one mineral from another. These properties are consistent, testable, and form the backbone of mineral identification.

In this lesson, you’ll learn the most important physical properties used in competitions, how to interpret them, and how they connect to a mineral’s internal structure.

2. Hardness

Hardness measures a mineral’s resistance to scratching. The Mohs Hardness Scale ranks minerals from 1 (softest) to 10 (hardest).

Mohs Scale (Key Examples)

  • 1 — Talc
  • 2 — Gypsum
  • 3 — Calcite
  • 5 — Apatite
  • 7 — Quartz
  • 10 — Diamond

Hardness is directly related to atomic bonding strength. Stronger bonds = harder mineral.

3. Luster

Luster describes how light reflects off a mineral’s surface. It is one of the fastest ways to narrow down possibilities.

Main Categories

  • Metallic — shiny like metal (e.g., pyrite, galena)
  • Nonmetallic — includes:
    • Vitreous (glassy)
    • Pearly
    • Silky
    • Earthy
    • Resinous

4. Streak

Streak is the color of a mineral’s powder when rubbed on a porcelain streak plate. It is often more reliable than surface color.

Important Notes

  • Metallic minerals usually have a dark streak.
  • Nonmetallic minerals often have a white or colorless streak.
  • Hematite’s streak is always red-brown — a classic ID clue.

5. Cleavage & Fracture

These describe how a mineral breaks.

Cleavage

Cleavage is the tendency to break along flat, parallel planes. It occurs when atomic bonds are weaker in certain directions.

Fracture

Fracture is irregular breakage. Types include:

  • Conchoidal — smooth, curved (e.g., quartz)
  • Uneven
  • Fibrous

6. Specific Gravity

Specific gravity measures density relative to water. It helps identify minerals with unusually high or low density.

Examples

  • Galena — very high SG (feels heavy)
  • Graphite — very low SG (feels light)

7. Other Useful Properties

  • Magnetism — magnetite
  • Reaction to acid — calcite fizzes with HCl
  • Fluorescence — some minerals glow under UV
  • Taste — halite tastes salty (only in controlled settings)
  • Smell — sulfur smells like rotten eggs

Vocabulary

    9. Summary

    Mineral identification relies on consistent, observable physical properties. Hardness, luster, streak, cleavage, fracture, and specific gravity form the core of competition-level mineral ID. Mastering these properties will make the rest of the Rocks & Minerals event significantly easier.
