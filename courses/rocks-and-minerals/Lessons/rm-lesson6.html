Lesson 6 — Metamorphic Rocks

MasterLab Rocks & Minerals (Division C)

1. Introduction

Metamorphic rocks form when existing rocks (igneous, sedimentary, or older metamorphic rocks) are changed by heat, pressure, and chemically active fluids. These changes occur without melting, producing new textures and minerals. In Science Olympiad, metamorphic rocks are heavily tested because their textures and mineral assemblages reveal the conditions under which they formed.

2. What Causes Metamorphism?

Three main factors drive metamorphism:

  • Heat — increases mineral mobility and recrystallization.
  • Pressure — compacts minerals and aligns them into layers.
  • Chemically active fluids — transport ions and promote new mineral growth.

These factors work together to transform the parent rock (protolith) into a new metamorphic rock.

3. Foliated Metamorphic Rocks

Foliation is the alignment of minerals into layers or bands due to directed pressure. Foliated rocks are extremely common in competition and follow a predictable progression.

Foliation Progression (Low → High Grade)

  • Slate — very fine‑grained; splits into flat sheets; dull surface.
  • Phyllite — silky sheen; wavy layers; slightly larger grains.
  • Schist — visible mica flakes; sparkly; strongly foliated.
  • Gneiss — banded appearance; alternating light and dark minerals.

This sequence is one of the most important metamorphic concepts in Science Olympiad.

4. Non‑Foliated Metamorphic Rocks

Non‑foliated rocks form when pressure is equal in all directions or when the rock is made of minerals that do not align easily.

Common Types

  • Marble — metamorphosed limestone; reacts with acid; softer than quartzite.
  • Quartzite — metamorphosed sandstone; very hard; scratches glass.
  • Anthracite Coal — shiny, hard coal; highest grade of coal metamorphism.
  • Hornfels — fine‑grained; forms in contact metamorphism zones.

5. Types of Metamorphism

Different geological settings produce different metamorphic textures and minerals.

Regional Metamorphism

  • Occurs over large areas during mountain building.
  • Produces foliated rocks like schist and gneiss.

Contact Metamorphism

  • Occurs when rocks are heated by nearby magma.
  • Produces non‑foliated rocks like hornfels and marble.

Hydrothermal Metamorphism

  • Hot, mineral‑rich fluids alter rock chemistry.
  • Common near mid‑ocean ridges and volcanic systems.

6. Index Minerals

Index minerals form only under specific temperature and pressure conditions. They help geologists determine metamorphic grade.

Important Index Minerals

  • Chlorite — low grade.
  • Biotite — low to medium grade.
  • Garnet — medium grade.
  • Staurolite — medium to high grade.
  • Kyanite — high pressure.
  • Sillimanite — high temperature.

7. Metamorphic Protoliths

Many metamorphic rocks can be identified by knowing their parent rock.

Common Protolith Pairs

  • Limestone → Marble
  • Sandstone → Quartzite
  • Shale → Slate → Phyllite → Schist → Gneiss
  • Bituminous Coal → Anthracite Coal

Vocabulary

    9. Summary

    Metamorphic rocks reveal the conditions under which they formed. Foliated rocks show directed pressure, while non‑foliated rocks form under uniform pressure or from minerals that do not align. Understanding metamorphic textures, index minerals, and protoliths allows you to quickly identify metamorphic rocks in competition.
