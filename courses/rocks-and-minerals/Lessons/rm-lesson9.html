Lesson 9 — Fossils, Geologic Time & Earth History

MasterLab Rocks & Minerals (Division C)

1. Introduction

Fossils and geologic time help scientists interpret Earth’s past. Although Rocks & Minerals focuses on identification, many tests include questions about fossils, dating methods, and major events in Earth history. This lesson gives you the essential background needed to understand how rocks record time.

2. What Are Fossils?

Fossils are preserved remains, impressions, or traces of ancient organisms. They form under specific conditions, usually involving rapid burial and low oxygen.

Types of Fossils

  • Body fossils — bones, shells, teeth, leaves.
  • Trace fossils — footprints, burrows, coprolites.
  • Permineralization — minerals fill pore spaces (common in bone and wood).
  • Casts & molds — impressions left in sediment.
  • Amber fossils — organisms trapped in tree resin.

3. How Fossils Form

Fossilization requires special conditions:

  • Rapid burial.
  • Low oxygen (reduces decay).
  • Hard parts (shells, bones) fossilize more easily.
  • Mineral-rich water promotes preservation.

Most fossils are found in sedimentary rocks.

4. The Geologic Time Scale

The geologic time scale divides Earth’s 4.6‑billion‑year history into eons, eras, periods, and epochs. It is based on major biological and geological events.

Major Divisions

  • Precambrian — earliest life, simple organisms.
  • Paleozoic — explosion of life, fish, amphibians.
  • Mesozoic — age of reptiles; dinosaurs dominate.
  • Cenozoic — age of mammals; humans appear.

Many Science Olympiad questions reference these eras.

5. Relative Dating

Relative dating determines the order of events without assigning exact ages.

Key Principles

  • Superposition — oldest layers at the bottom.
  • Original horizontality — sediments are deposited flat.
  • Cross‑cutting relationships — intrusions/faults are younger.
  • Inclusions — fragments inside a rock are older than the rock itself.

6. Absolute Dating

Absolute dating assigns numerical ages using radioactive decay.

Common Methods

  • Uranium‑Lead — used for very old rocks.
  • Potassium‑Argon — volcanic rocks.
  • Carbon‑14 — recent organic remains (up to ~50,000 years).

Absolute dating is often used alongside relative dating.

7. Index Fossils

Index fossils help correlate rock layers across large distances.

Characteristics of Good Index Fossils

  • Widespread geographically.
  • Short time range.
  • Easy to recognize.

Examples

  • Trilobites — Paleozoic.
  • Ammonites — Mesozoic.
  • Foraminifera — various ages.

8. Major Earth History Events

Several major events appear frequently in Science Olympiad tests.

Important Events

  • Cambrian Explosion — rapid diversification of life.
  • Permian Extinction — largest mass extinction.
  • K‑Pg Extinction — dinosaurs go extinct.
  • Formation of Pangaea — supercontinent assembly.
  • Rise of mammals — Cenozoic dominance.

    10. Summary

    Fossils and geologic time provide a framework for understanding Earth’s history. Relative and absolute dating methods help determine the age of rocks, while index fossils allow correlation across regions. These concepts often appear in Science Olympiad tests and help connect rock identification to larger geological processes.
