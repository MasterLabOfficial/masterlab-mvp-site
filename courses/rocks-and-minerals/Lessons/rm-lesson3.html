Lesson 3 — Mineral Chemical Properties & Composition

MasterLab Rocks & Minerals (Division C)

1. Introduction

You’ve learned what a mineral is and how to use physical properties to identify it. Now it’s time to go deeper — into the **chemical side** of minerals. Chemical composition explains why minerals have certain colors, densities, hardness values, and even how they form.

In this lesson, you’ll learn how to read mineral formulas, understand major mineral groups, and connect chemistry to the properties you see in competition.

2. Chemical Formulas of Minerals

Every true mineral has a **definite chemical composition**, which can be written as a formula. These formulas show which elements are present and in what ratios.

Examples

  • Quartz — SiO₂
  • Halite — NaCl
  • Calcite — CaCO₃
  • Hematite — Fe₂O₃
  • Galena — PbS

Some minerals allow limited substitution of elements (solid solution), but the overall composition stays within a narrow range.

3. Silicate vs Non‑Silicate Minerals

Most of Earth’s crust is made of **silicate minerals**, which are built around the silicon‑oxygen tetrahedron (SiO₄)⁴⁻. Non‑silicates include all other mineral groups.

Silicates

Silicates are classified by how their tetrahedra are arranged:

  • Isolated tetrahedra (olivine)
  • Single chains (pyroxenes)
  • Double chains (amphiboles)
  • Sheets (micas, clays)
  • Frameworks (quartz, feldspars)

Non‑Silicates

Non‑silicate groups include:

  • Carbonates — CO₃²⁻ (e.g., calcite, dolomite)
  • Sulfates — SO₄²⁻ (e.g., gypsum)
  • Sulfides — S²⁻ with metals (e.g., pyrite, galena)
  • Oxides — O²⁻ with metals (e.g., hematite, magnetite)
  • Halides — Cl⁻, F⁻, etc. (e.g., halite, fluorite)
  • Native elements — single elements (e.g., gold, copper, sulfur)

4. Ionic vs Covalent Bonding in Minerals

The type of chemical bonding inside a mineral strongly affects its properties.

Ionic Bonds

Ionic bonds form between positively and negatively charged ions. Many minerals, especially halides and some oxides, are primarily ionically bonded.

  • Often result in moderate hardness
  • Can be easily dissolved or altered
  • Examples: halite (NaCl), fluorite (CaF₂)

Covalent Bonds

Covalent bonds involve shared electrons. They are usually very strong.

  • Often result in very hard minerals
  • High melting points
  • Examples: diamond (pure C), quartz (SiO₂)

5. Solid Solution & Element Substitution

In some minerals, ions of similar size and charge can substitute for each other without changing the overall structure. This is called a **solid solution**.

Example: Olivine Series

Olivine ranges between:

  • Forsterite — Mg₂SiO₄
  • Fayalite — Fe₂SiO₄

Magnesium and iron can substitute for each other, creating a continuous range of compositions.

6. How Chemistry Affects Color

Color in minerals is often controlled by trace elements or defects in the crystal structure.

Examples

  • Amethyst — purple quartz colored by iron and radiation.
  • Malachite — green due to copper.
  • Azurite — blue due to copper.

Because color can vary, it is usually less reliable than other properties, but knowing the chemistry behind it can still help in competition.

7. Chemical Reactions of Minerals

Some minerals react chemically in ways that are useful for identification.

Classic Example: Carbonates and Acid

Carbonate minerals (like calcite) react with dilute hydrochloric acid (HCl) to produce bubbles of CO₂ gas.

  • Calcite — strong fizz in cold, dilute HCl.
  • Dolomite — weak fizz, stronger when powdered or warmed.
