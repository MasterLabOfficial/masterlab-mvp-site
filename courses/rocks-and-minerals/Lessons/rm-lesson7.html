Lesson 7 — The Rock Cycle & Geological Processes

MasterLab Rocks & Minerals (Division C)

1. Introduction

The rock cycle describes how rocks change from one type to another through natural geological processes. Igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rocks are all connected through heat, pressure, melting, weathering, erosion, and deposition. Understanding these processes is essential for interpreting Earth’s history and answering process‑based questions in Science Olympiad.

2. The Three Rock Types

All rocks fall into one of three categories:

  • Igneous — formed from cooled magma or lava.
  • Sedimentary — formed from sediments or chemical/biological processes.
  • Metamorphic — formed from heat, pressure, or chemically active fluids.

The rock cycle explains how each type can transform into another.

3. Weathering & Erosion

Weathering breaks down rocks at Earth’s surface, while erosion transports the resulting sediments to new locations.

Types of Weathering

  • Mechanical — physical breakdown (ice wedging, abrasion).
  • Chemical — chemical alteration (dissolution, oxidation).
  • Biological — caused by organisms (roots, acids from plants).

Weathering and erosion are the first steps in forming sedimentary rocks.

4. Deposition, Compaction & Cementation

After sediments are transported, they settle (deposition), are buried (compaction), and become glued together by minerals (cementation). Together, these processes form sedimentary rocks.

Common Cements

  • Calcite
  • Quartz
  • Iron oxides

5. Heat & Pressure

When rocks are buried deep within Earth or subjected to tectonic forces, they experience increased heat and pressure. These conditions cause minerals to recrystallize and align, forming metamorphic rocks.

Key Concepts

  • Low‑grade metamorphism — slight changes (slate, phyllite).
  • High‑grade metamorphism — major changes (schist, gneiss).
  • Contact metamorphism — heat from magma.
  • Regional metamorphism — pressure from mountain building.

6. Melting & Cooling

If rocks are heated enough to melt, they form magma. When magma cools, it becomes igneous rock again, completing the cycle.

Cooling Types

  • Slow cooling → intrusive rocks (granite, gabbro).
  • Fast cooling → extrusive rocks (basalt, rhyolite).
  • Very rapid cooling → glassy rocks (obsidian).

7. The Full Rock Cycle

The rock cycle is not a simple loop — any rock type can transform into any other rock type depending on the processes involved.

Examples

  • Granite → weathering → sand → sandstone → quartzite.
  • Basalt → heat/pressure → amphibolite.
  • Limestone → metamorphism → marble.
  • Shale → burial → slate → schist → gneiss.

These transformations are frequently tested in Science Olympiad.

8. Geological Processes

Several large‑scale processes drive the rock cycle:

  • Plate tectonics — creates mountains, volcanoes, and metamorphic zones.
  • Volcanism — produces igneous rocks.
  • Uplift — exposes rocks to weathering.
  • Subduction — recycles rocks into the mantle.

Vocabulary

    10. Summary

    The rock cycle explains how igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rocks form and transform through geological processes. Understanding these pathways helps you interpret Earth’s history and answer process‑based questions in competition.
