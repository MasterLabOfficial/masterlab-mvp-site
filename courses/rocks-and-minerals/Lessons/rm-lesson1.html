Lesson 1 — What Is a Mineral?

MasterLab Rocks & Minerals (Division C)

1. Introduction

Before you can identify minerals, classify rocks, or understand the rock cycle, you need to know what a mineral actually is. This seems simple — but in Science Olympiad, precision matters. A mineral is not just “a rock” or “a crystal.” It has a strict scientific definition, and every part of that definition is testable.

In this lesson, you’ll learn the **five criteria** that define a mineral, explore common misconceptions, and build the foundation for all future lessons.

2. The Five Criteria of a Mineral

A substance must meet all five of these criteria to be considered a mineral:

  1. Naturally Occurring — Not made by humans.
  2. Inorganic — Not made by living organisms.
  3. Solid — Must be solid at standard temperature and pressure.
  4. Definite Chemical Composition — Has a specific chemical formula.
  5. Ordered Internal Structure — Atoms arranged in a repeating pattern (crystalline).

Why each criterion matters

1. Naturally Occurring
Substances created in a lab (e.g., synthetic diamonds) are not minerals, even if they look identical.

2. Inorganic
Anything produced by life — shells, pearls, wood — is excluded.

3. Solid
Mercury is a metal, but because it is liquid at room temperature, it is not a mineral.

4. Definite Chemical Composition
Quartz is always SiO₂. Halite is always NaCl. This consistency is key for identification.

5. Ordered Internal Structure
This is what gives minerals their crystal shapes, cleavage, and many physical properties.

3. Common Non‑Minerals (Trick Examples)

Science Olympiad loves to test your understanding by giving you substances that look like minerals but fail one or more criteria.

  • Obsidian — volcanic glass; no crystal structure.
  • Coal — organic; formed from plant matter.
  • Pearl — organic; produced by oysters.
  • Amber — fossilized tree resin; organic.
  • Opal — lacks a true crystalline structure (amorphous).

These appear constantly in competitions — knowing why they are not minerals gives you a huge advantage.

4. Key Vocabulary

  • Crystalline — atoms arranged in a repeating pattern.
  • Amorphous — lacking a crystal structure.
  • Inorganic — not derived from living organisms.
  • Chemical Formula — representation of a substance’s composition.
  • Mineraloid — mineral‑like substance lacking one criterion (e.g., opal).

5. Summary

A mineral is a naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and an ordered internal structure. If a substance fails even one of these criteria, it is not a mineral.

This definition is the foundation for everything else in Rocks & Minerals — identification, classification, and competition strategy.
