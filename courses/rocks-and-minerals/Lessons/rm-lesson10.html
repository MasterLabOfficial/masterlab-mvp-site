Lesson 10 — Plate Tectonics, Earth Structure & Mineral Formation Environments

MasterLab Rocks & Minerals (Division C)

1. Introduction

Plate tectonics is the engine that drives Earth’s geology. It controls where rocks form, where minerals concentrate, and how Earth’s crust evolves over time. Understanding tectonic environments helps explain why certain minerals and rocks appear together and how major geological features develop.

2. Earth’s Internal Structure

Earth is divided into layers based on composition and physical properties.

Compositional Layers

  • Crust — continental and oceanic.
  • Mantle — ultramafic rock (peridotite).
  • Core — iron and nickel.

Mechanical Layers

  • Lithosphere — rigid crust + upper mantle.
  • Asthenosphere — plastic, partially molten mantle.
  • Mesosphere — lower mantle.
  • Outer core — liquid metal.
  • Inner core — solid metal.

3. Plate Boundaries

Plate boundaries are where most geological activity occurs.

Divergent Boundaries

  • Plates move apart.
  • Forms mid‑ocean ridges and rift valleys.
  • Produces basaltic magma.

Convergent Boundaries

  • Plates collide.
  • Subduction zones create volcanic arcs.
  • High pressure → metamorphism (blueschist, eclogite).

Transform Boundaries

  • Plates slide past each other.
  • Cause earthquakes (e.g., San Andreas Fault).

4. How Magma Forms

Magma forms in three main ways:

  • Decompression melting — at divergent boundaries.
  • Flux melting — water lowers melting point at subduction zones.
  • Heat transfer — rising magma melts surrounding rock.

These processes determine the composition of igneous rocks.

5. Mineral Formation Environments

Minerals form in specific geological settings, each producing characteristic minerals.

Hydrothermal Veins

  • Hot, mineral‑rich fluids deposit ore minerals.
  • Common minerals: quartz, galena, chalcopyrite, sphalerite.

Pegmatites

  • Extremely coarse‑grained igneous rocks.
  • Contain rare minerals: tourmaline, beryl, spodumene.

Evaporite Basins

  • Water evaporates → minerals precipitate.
  • Halite, gypsum, sylvite.

Metamorphic Zones

  • High pressure/temperature environments.
  • Garnet, staurolite, kyanite, sillimanite.

6. Ore Deposits & Tectonics

Many ore deposits form in predictable tectonic settings.

Examples

  • Porphyry copper deposits — subduction zones.
  • Volcanogenic massive sulfides (VMS) — mid‑ocean ridges.
  • Placer deposits — rivers concentrate heavy minerals (gold, magnetite).
  • Banded iron formations — ancient oceans with rising oxygen.

7. Earthquakes & Volcanoes

Earthquakes and volcanoes are direct results of plate movement.

Earthquakes

  • Occur along faults.
  • Most common at plate boundaries.
  • Measured using the moment magnitude scale.

Volcanoes

  • Form at divergent and convergent boundaries.
  • Hotspots create intraplate volcanoes (Hawaii).
  • Magma composition controls eruption style.

8. Geologic Map Interpretation

Many Science Olympiad tests include map‑based questions.

Key Skills

  • Recognizing rock types and ages.
  • Identifying faults and folds.
  • Interpreting cross‑sections.
  • Understanding depositional environments.

9. Key Vocabulary

  • Plate tectonics — movement of Earth’s lithospheric plates.
  • Subduction — one plate sinks beneath another.
  • Asthenosphere — partially molten mantle layer.
  • Hydrothermal — involving hot, mineral‑rich fluids.
  • Pegmatite — extremely coarse‑grained igneous rock.
  • Ore deposit — concentration of valuable minerals.

10. Summary

Plate tectonics controls where rocks and minerals form, how they change, and how Earth’s crust evolves. Understanding tectonic settings, mineral formation environments, and geological processes ties together everything you’ve learned in this course and prepares you for advanced interpretation questions in Science Olympiad.
